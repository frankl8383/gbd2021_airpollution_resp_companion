# Abstract Main Text Draft v3

## Abstract

### Background

Age-standardized trends alone may misrepresent East Asia's air pollution-attributable respiratory burden because demographic aging and vulnerability shifts can alter counts. We examined PM2.5-attributable chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), PM2.5-attributable lower respiratory infections (LRI), and ozone-attributable COPD using Global Burden of Disease 2021 estimates.

### Methods

We analyzed Global Burden of Disease 2021 estimates for China, Japan, Republic of Korea, East Asia, Global, and high Sociodemographic Index (High SDI), 1990-2021. Analyses combined attributable burden, total burden, and population data with constrained log-linear breakpoint comparison of age-standardized series, four-factor accounting decomposition of burden-count change into population growth, aging, underlying disease-rate change, and population attributable fraction (PAF) change, and age-sex vulnerability profiling. Sensitivity analyses repeated the assessment for `1990–2019`, compared raw and capped implied PAF, used annual-chain decomposition, and reran COPD-death decomposition on the common age set after excluding structurally empty `<15` cells.

### Results

Under prespecified 0-versus-1-break comparisons, all 36 age-standardized series preferred one breakpoint. PM2.5-attributable COPD age-standardized death rates in China and East Asia shifted from increase to decline after a 2009 breakpoint, while ozone-attributable COPD age-standardized death rates shifted around 2007. Aging pushed COPD burden counts upward in China and East Asia, whereas underlying disease-rate change pushed downward; PAF change amplified PM2.5-attributable COPD increases but reinforced decline for ozone-attributable COPD disability-adjusted life years. For PM2.5-attributable LRI deaths, the under-5 share fell from 67.2% to 2.9% in China and from 66.2% to 2.8% in East Asia, while the global comparator remained higher in 2021 (23.1%). By 2021, the age-70-plus group dominated COPD disability-adjusted life year burden in East Asia for both PM2.5 and ozone. Sensitivity analyses preserved the main qualitative patterns, capped versus uncapped PAF gave identical results, and the common-age-set rerun preserved COPD-death net changes, with the largest differences concentrated in population-growth and aging components.

### Conclusions

East Asia's respiratory burden transition cannot be interpreted from age-standardized decline alone. Net attributable burden change reflected temporal inflection, demographic aging, underlying total disease-rate dynamics, PAF change, and concentration of residual burden in older age groups.
